About Mock Trial

Mock Trial is an academic competition led by the Constitutional Rights Foundation (CRF), where students all over LA County learn a hypothetical criminal case and prepare to put on a simulation of a bench trial held in Downtown LA. Join Mock Trial to:

• Learn about criminal law!

• Gain public speaking skills!

• Work collaboratively!

• Stand out to colleges in the application process!

Click here to visit CRF's website!

Roles

• 6 Trial Attorneys: conducts direct and cross examinations, delivers opening statements and closing arguments (most commitment)

• 2 Pretrial Attorneys: argues constitutional issues using precedents

• 8 Witnesses: plays roles of hired experts and people involved with the crime

• 1 Bailiff: calls the court to order, swears in witnesses

• 1 Clerk: keeps time for the court

Parts of a Trial

A typical case has two sides: the prosecution and the defense. The job of the prosecution is to do justice by arguing that the defendant (person who is on trial) has committed the crime that the defendant is accused of. The job of the defense is to show that their client, the defendant, is innocent. The two sides convince the judge or jury that their side is correct by delivering opening statements and closing arguments, which are speeches that frame the story or the case for the court, and calling up and questioning witnesses. The witnesses may be experts who were hired by either side (i.e. a handwriting expert hired to analyze handwritten evidence left on the scene), law enforcement officers who were involved in the invesigation (usually a detective or police officer on the prosecution), and regular people who are familiar with the victim or the defendant. The two sides may also introduce evidence, such as diagrams of the crime scene or physical evidence uncovered at the scene, which are often argued and analyzed by professional witnesses.

Pretrial

The pretrial motion is only conducted in high school mock trial competitions. In a pretrial motion, the defense presents first. What the defense is arguing for changes from year to year with the case, but they will usually argue that a potential piece of evidence should be excluded due to some legal precedents. A legal precedent is a prior court case with a verdict that can be used as an example in future, similar cases. The two sides are given a list of possible precedent cases, and they must create a 4 minute statement with those regarding whether or not the evidence is permissible. During the pretrial motion, the judge may also ask questions about those precedents, so it's important that the pretrial attorney is familiar with all of the cases. After both sides have presented, each side gets a 2 minutes rebuttal, during which the judge may continue asking questions.

Examples:

• Defense Pretrial (CA State Finals 2018): 4:30 to 8:50
• Prosecution Pretrial (CA State Finals 2018): 8:50 to 13:35
• Defense Pretrial Rebuttal (CA State Finals 2018): 13:40 to 15:00
• Prosecution Pretrial Rebuttal (CA State Finals 2018): 15:00 to 17:10
